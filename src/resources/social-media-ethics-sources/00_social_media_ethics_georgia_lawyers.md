SOCIAL MEDIA AND THE LAWYER-CLIENT RELATIONSHIP: ETHICAL BOUNDARIES FOR GEORGIA ATTORNEYS

By Ivy | For Review — Dominant Law

---

INTRODUCTION

The rise of social media has created a minefield of ethical obligations for attorneys. What a lawyer advises a client about their social media presence can mean the difference between a successful case outcome and a disciplinary proceeding. This article examines the ethical dimensions of a lawyer's role in guiding clients through social media usage, with specific focus on Georgia Rules of Professional Conduct, Georgia Formal Advisory Opinions, and Georgia case law.

This article is for review purposes only and does not constitute legal advice.

I. THE FOUNDATIONAL RULES: GEORGIA RULES OF PROFESSIONAL CONDUCT RELEVANT TO SOCIAL MEDIA

The State Bar of Georgia's Rules of Professional Conduct govern attorney conduct in Georgia. The following rules are most directly implicated by social media advice and use:

• Rule 1.1 — Competence: A lawyer must provide competent representation, which includes understanding the benefits and risks associated with relevant technology, including social media platforms and their privacy settings.

• Rule 1.6 — Confidentiality of Information: A lawyer shall not reveal information relating to the representation of a client unless the client gives informed consent. This applies to all communications, including social media posts that may inadvertently disclose client information.

• Rule 1.7 — Conflict of Interest: General Rule: A lawyer shall not represent a client if the representation involves a concurrent conflict of interest. Social media connections with adverse parties can trigger conflicts.

• Rule 4.2 — Communication with Person Represented by Counsel: In representing a client, a lawyer shall not communicate about the subject matter of the representation with a person the lawyer knows to be represented by another lawyer in the matter. This prohibits social media contact with represented parties.

• Rule 4.3 — Dealing with Unrepresented Person: When communicating with an unrepresented person, the lawyer shall not give the person legal advice except to secure counsel.

• Rule 7.1 — Communications Concerning a Lawyer's Services: A lawyer shall not make false or misleading communications about the lawyer or the lawyer's services. This applies to all firm social media content.

• Rule 8.4 — Misconduct: It is professional misconduct for a lawyer to violate the Rules of Professional Conduct or to engage in conduct that is prejudicial to the administration of justice.

II. GEORGIA FORMAL ADVISORY OPINIONS BEARING ON SOCIAL MEDIA AND CLIENT ADVICE

A. Formal Advisory Opinion FAO 20-1 (2022)

In 2022, the Supreme Court of Georgia approved Formal Advisory Opinion No. 20-1, addressing the scope of Rule 4.2 in the context of communications with former employees of represented organizations. The opinion confirmed that Rule 4.2 "only addresses a lawyer's ability to communicate about the subject matter of a representation with a person who is represented by counsel in the matter." While this opinion focused on organizational representation rather than individual social media contact, it establishes Georgia's framework for evaluating lawyer communications online. The opinion clarified that anti-contact rules apply specifically to represented persons, not all contacts in the digital space.

B. Formal Advisory Opinion FAO 22-1 (2024)

The Supreme Court of Georgia approved Formal Advisory Opinion No. 22-1 in 2024, addressing remote legal services and jurisdiction. While primarily addressing multi-jurisdictional practice, the opinion signals the Court's recognition that technology-mediated legal services require updated ethical frameworks. Attorneys providing social media advice to clients across state lines should be mindful of this guidance.

III. WHAT A LAWYER MAY ADVISE A CLIENT TO DO

Based on the Georgia Rules and applicable ethics opinions from multiple jurisdictions, the following advisements are generally permissible:

A. Adjust Privacy Settings

Bar associations including the New York City Bar and DC Bar have concluded that attorneys may advise clients to change the privacy settings on their social media accounts. Georgia attorneys may similarly advise clients to limit who can view existing content, as this controls access to already-published information rather than instructing the client to create new content or alter evidence.

B. Refrain from Posting About the Case

Lawyers may advise clients to avoid posting anything about their case, witnesses, injuries, or legal strategy. This falls within permissible guidance on protecting the client's own interests and is consistent with the duty of competence under Rule 1.1.

C. Avoid Contact with Opposing Parties

A lawyer may advise a client not to interact with the opposing party or their representatives on social media, consistent with general litigation strategy and Rule 4.2's anti-contact principles.

D. Set Separate Professional and Personal Accounts

Where appropriate, lawyers may guide clients on creating separate professional social media presences distinct from personal accounts, particularly in cases where the client's personal brand may be relevant to their claim (e.g., a public figure or small business owner).

---

IV. WHAT A LAWYER MAY NOT ADVISE OR DO

A. Instructing Clients to "Clean Up" Social Media Misleadingly

A lawyer cannot advise a client to delete content in a way that amounts to spoliation of evidence or that misleads the court. If the content is relevant to litigation, deleting it may constitute obstruction in violation of Rule 8.4 and potentially criminal statutes.

B. Contacting Represented Parties on Social Media

Friending, messaging, or otherwise communicating with a represented party on social media without authorization from their counsel violates Rule 4.2 in most jurisdictions, including Georgia.

C. Providing Substantive Legal Advice on Public Forums

Lawyers who respond to questions on social media in a way that constitutes specific legal advice to a prospective client risk creating an attorney-client relationship under Rule 1.18 (Duties to Prospective Client), which may trigger confidentiality and conflict obligations.

D. Revealing Confidential Information

Under Rule 1.6, lawyers must protect all information relating to the representation of a client. A seemingly innocuous post such as "Just settled a motor vehicle accident case for a client in DeKalb County" can violate this rule if it identifies the client or reveals case details.

E. Misrepresenting Qualifications or Results on Firm Social Media

Rule 7.1 prohibits false or misleading communications about a lawyer's services. Testimonials, case results, and peer credentials on social media must be accurate and not misleading.

V. GEORGIA CASE LAW AND DISCIPLINARY REFERENCES

A. In re Formal Advisory Opinion No. 20-1 (Ga. 2022)

The Supreme Court of Georgia addressed the scope of Rule 4.2 and confirmed the anti-contact rule applies only to communications about the subject matter of representation with represented persons. The Court emphasized that lawyers must know the status of the person they are contacting before engaging on any platform, including social media.

B. In re Formal Advisory Opinion No. 22-1 (Ga. 2024)

The Court addressed remote legal services and held that a Georgia lawyer may provide services by remote means with no relationship to Georgia other than the lawyer's physical location, provided the lawyer does not hold out as licensed in Georgia and takes reasonable steps to prevent misunderstandings about licensure.

C. Disciplinary Cases Involving Social Media

Across jurisdictions, attorneys have faced discipline for social media misuse. In one notable case, two attorneys claimed unfamiliarity with Facebook's privacy settings as a defense, but were found to have acted in a manner inconsistent with their ethical obligations. While not a Georgia case, this illustrates the risk of ignorance of platform functionality — directly implicating Rule 1.1's competence requirement.

Georgia attorneys are subject to discipline for violations of the Rules of Professional Conduct under Part IV, Chapter 1 of the Bar Rules, with penalties ranging from reprimand to disbarment under Rule 4-102.

VI. BEST PRACTICES FOR GEORGIA LAWYERS ADVISING ON SOCIAL MEDIA

1. Issue written social media guidelines to clients at the outset of engagement, clearly outlining what they may and may not post.

2. Document all social media advice given in the client file.

3. Stay current with Georgia Formal Advisory Opinions and State Bar ethics guidance on technology and social media.

4. Do not give substantive legal advice through public social media posts, as this may create attorney-client relationships and confidentiality obligations.

5. Be aware of the limits of your competence — if unfamiliar with a platform's privacy settings, consult a technology advisor or refer to available ethics guidance.

6. Ensure all firm social media communications comply with Rules 7.1 and 8.4 and Georgia's Rules for Legal Advertising.

7. Consult the State Bar of Georgia's Office of General Counsel Ethics Helpline at 404-527-8741 with specific questions.

---

VII. SOCIAL MEDIA AND THE LAWYER'S OWN PRESENCE

Beyond client advice, lawyers must regulate their own social media conduct:

• Ensure all firm communications about services are truthful and not misleading (Rule 7.1).

• Avoid creating conflicts of interest through social media connections with opposing parties or their counsel (Rule 1.7).

• Do not research jurors or potential jurors on social media in ways that violate Rule 3.5.

• Be mindful that social media connections with prospective clients can give rise to implied attorney-client relationships (Rule 1.18).

---

CONCLUSION

The intersection of social media and legal ethics continues to evolve, and Georgia attorneys must navigate an increasingly complex landscape. The core obligations — competence (Rule 1.1), confidentiality (Rule 1.6), honesty (Rule 8.4), and avoiding conflicts (Rule 1.7) — remain constant, but their application to social media requires ongoing attention and technological literacy. Attorneys who proactively advise clients on social media boundaries, stay current with Georgia Formal Advisory Opinions and Rules of Professional Conduct, and document their advice will be best positioned to avoid disciplinary pitfalls while leveraging social platforms effectively.
